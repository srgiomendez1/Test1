# 🟡 DIO2 — Thyroid T4→T3 Conversion Impairment

| Gene | DIO2 (Iodothyronine Deiodinase 2) |
|------|-----------------------------------|
| rsID | rs12885300 |
| Your Genotype | T/T |
| Classification | Homozygous for Thr92Ala variant |
| Chromosome | 14 |

> **Note:** For rs12885300, T on the + strand corresponds to the Ala92 (risk) allele based on prevailing literature interpretation. T/T = homozygous Thr92Ala. If you have thyroid symptoms despite normal TSH, this variant is the most likely explanation.

## What This Gene Does

DIO2 converts **inactive T4 (thyroxine)** into **active T3 (triiodothyronine)** inside cells — particularly in the brain, pituitary, muscle, heart, and brown adipose tissue. This conversion is separate from what the thyroid gland measures; you can have a "normal" TSH while still having poor cellular T3 availability if DIO2 is impaired.

## What T/T Means for You

The Thr92Ala variant reduces intracellular DIO2 enzyme activity, particularly in:
- **Cartilage and joint tissue** — impaired T3 conversion here is the primary mechanism for the DIO2-osteoarthritis association (OR ~1.3–1.5 in large GWAS)
- **Brain** — reduced T3 in brain tissue can cause cognitive slowing, brain fog, and mood changes even with normal TSH
- **Muscle** — lower T3 impairs mitochondrial energy production in muscle cells

**The "euthyroid sick" problem:** You can have a completely normal TSH (the pituitary's report on thyroid hormone levels in blood) while your tissues are relatively T3-deficient because DIO2 isn't converting efficiently inside cells. This is why many physicians miss this.

**Combined with your HLA autoimmune profile:** Your HLA shared epitope raises risk for Hashimoto's thyroiditis, which can trigger anti-thyroid antibodies. DIO2 then compounds the problem by reducing conversion even of whatever T4 the thyroid does produce.

## Protocol

### Lab Monitoring (Critical — Request All)
Standard doctors order only TSH. For your DIO2 variant, you need the **full picture**:

| Test | Optimal Target | Why |
|------|---------------|-----|
| TSH | 0.5–2.0 mIU/L | Tighter range than standard (0.4–4.5); aim for lower half |
| Free T4 | Mid-to-upper normal | Measures thyroid output |
| **Free T3** | **Upper quartile of normal** | THIS is the key — DIO2 may show normal T4 but low T3 |
| Reverse T3 (rT3) | < 15 ng/dL | High rT3 competes with T3 at receptors; indicates conversion problem |
| TPO antibodies | Negative | Autoimmune activity check (HLA risk) |
| TGAb | Negative | Thyroglobulin antibodies |

**If Free T3 is low-normal with normal TSH:** This is DIO2 impairment in action. Discuss T3 supplementation options with an integrative or functional medicine doctor (desiccated thyroid, liothyronine, or T4+T3 combination).

### Nutritional Support
- **Selenium (selenomethionine) 150–200 mcg/day** — co-factor for deiodinase enzymes; Brazil nuts (1–2/day = 150 mcg) or supplement
- **Iodine: eat seafood 2–3x/week** — thyroid requires iodine; get it from food (sardines, salmon, seaweed/nori); avoid mega-dose iodine supplements
- **Zinc 15–25 mg/day** — required for T4→T3 conversion and thyroid hormone receptor binding
- **Iron** — check ferritin; iron deficiency impairs thyroid function. Target 50–100 ng/mL ferritin.
- **Vitamin D3 4,000 IU/day** — low D dramatically increases autoimmune thyroid risk (HLA overlap)

### Joint Protection (DIO2-Osteoarthritis Link)
- Low-impact cardio preferred (cycling, swimming, elliptical)
- Anti-inflammatory diet directly reduces cartilage DIO2 burden
- Collagen peptides 10–15 g/day — supports cartilage matrix
