> **Accuracy Note:** The original report categorized this under "Heart Disease [Iron]" and recommended monitoring ferritin. This framing is misleading. SOD2 rs4880 is a **mitochondrial antioxidant** gene — it neutralizes superoxide radicals generated during normal cellular energy production. The iron-cardiomyopathy connection found in studies was specifically in patients with **hereditary hemochromatosis (HH)**, a separate genetic condition causing iron overload. In the general population without HH, Val/Val primarily means increased susceptibility to mitochondrial oxidative stress, not iron dysregulation. The ferritin monitoring advice from the original report is not well-grounded for this variant specifically. This has been corrected.

## What This Gene Does

SOD2 encodes manganese superoxide dismutase (MnSOD), which sits inside **mitochondria** and neutralizes superoxide radicals — the primary reactive oxygen species generated during ATP (energy) production. It is the mitochondria's front-line antioxidant defense.

The Val16Ala (c.47T>C) variant affects the **mitochondrial targeting sequence** — the signal peptide that guides the protein into mitochondria:
- **Val16 (A on + strand, your genotype):** Disrupts the alpha-helix of the targeting sequence → protein is retained at the inner mitochondrial membrane rather than fully translocated → **30–40% lower SOD2 activity**
- **Ala16 (G on + strand):** Maintains helical structure → efficient import → full enzyme activity

## What the SOD2 Val/Val Means for You

- Your mitochondria produce superoxide at the same rate as anyone else, but neutralize it ~30–40% less efficiently
- This leads to **higher baseline mitochondrial oxidative stress**
- Over time, this accelerates mitochondrial DNA damage and reduces cellular energy efficiency
- Associated with: increased risk of diabetic complications (nephropathy), worse outcomes in obstructive sleep apnea, some cardiovascular disease risk in specific contexts

**The iron connection, clarified:** Excess free iron generates superoxide via the Fenton reaction. If you happen to have high iron levels, your reduced SOD2 activity means that iron-generated superoxide causes more damage. This is why the original report mentioned ferritin monitoring — but this is a secondary, conditional concern, not a primary iron metabolism problem.

## Actionable Protocol

**Primary intervention — reduce mitochondrial oxidative stress:**
- **CoQ10 (Ubiquinol form)** — 100–200 mg/day with fat; ubiquinol is both an electron carrier and a mitochondrial antioxidant; directly compensates for reduced SOD2 defense
- **MitoQ** — a mitochondrially-targeted CoQ10 analog with more direct evidence for SOD2 compensation (available as a supplement)
- **Magnesium glycinate** — 200–400 mg/day; required co-factor for hundreds of mitochondrial enzymes
- **NAC / Glutathione** — supports the broader cellular antioxidant network that SOD2 feeds into

**Exercise and mitochondrial biogenesis:**
- Aerobic exercise (Zone 2) is the most powerful intervention for improving mitochondrial density and antioxidant capacity — your ACTN3 endurance profile aligns perfectly here
- Exercise increases SOD2 expression in trained muscle tissue over time

**Dietary antioxidant support:**
- Cruciferous vegetables (sulforaphane → Nrf2 activation → upregulates antioxidant enzymes)
- Berries (anthocyanins directly quench mitochondrial ROS)
- Avoid: excess alcohol (disrupts mitochondrial electron transport chain)

**On iron and ferritin:**
- Monitor ferritin as a general health marker (target 50–100 ng/mL), but not as a primary concern from this variant specifically
- If ferritin is elevated (> 150 ng/mL), reducing iron load via blood donation or black tea with meals is reasonable
