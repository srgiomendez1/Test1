# 🟡 Wnt16 — Reduced Bone Density

|------|------------------------------------------------|
| rsID | rs9525638 |
| Your Genotype | T/T (confirmed AncestryDNA) |
| Classification | +/+ |
| Chromosome | 7 |
| Interaction | Compounds with DIO2 joint risk + FAM9B testosterone |

## What This Means

Wnt16 is a key regulator of bone formation and cortical bone thickness. The T/T variant is associated with:
- Reduced cortical bone density (the dense outer shell of bones)
- Increased fracture risk, particularly at the radius (forearm) and hip
- Reduced response to the normal bone-building signals from loading/exercise

This is compounded by:
- **Testosterone susceptibility (FAM9B)** — testosterone is a primary anabolic signal for bone
- **DIO2 thyroid-joint variant** — thyroid optimization also drives bone maintenance
- **CYP2R1 Vitamin D variant** — inadequate D3 impairs calcium absorption into bone

## Protocol

**The exercise prescription (most critical):**
- **Deadlifts** — described specifically in report for this variant; vertical spinal loading is the most effective mechanostimulus for bone density at the spine and hips
- Progressive overload: increase weight incrementally over months. Bone responds to increasing mechanical stress.
- Weight-bearing cardio (walking, hiking, stair climbing) supplements gym work
- Target: 3–4 strength sessions per week

**Supplements:**
- **Vitamin D3** — 2,000–10,000 IU/day with K2 (MK-7, 100–200 mcg/day). D3 drives calcium absorption; K2 directs calcium into bone rather than arteries. You need this for CYP2R1 as well.
- **Calcium** — from food (sardines, greens, almonds) rather than supplements where possible; if supplementing, use calcium citrate 500 mg (not carbonate) with food
- **Magnesium glycinate** — 200–400 mg/day; required co-factor for bone mineralization; most people are deficient
- **Boron** — 3–6 mg/day; increases bone mineral density in multiple studies
- **Collagen peptides** — 10–15 g/day; provides the protein matrix into which minerals are deposited

**Optimize testosterone** (see [FAM9B file](FAM9B-testosterone.md)) — low testosterone directly causes bone loss

**Avoid bone-depleting factors:**
- Excessive alcohol (inhibits osteoblast function)
- Smoking (reduces bone blood supply)
- High-dose caffeine > 4 cups/day (modestly reduces calcium absorption)
- Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) long-term (impair calcium absorption)
